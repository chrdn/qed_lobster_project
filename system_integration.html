System Level Sensitivity Analysis

While performing sensitivity analysis, we found multiple points of sensitivity.

  • Lobster license tier and boat size both influence lobster yield
  • Crew cut had a great impact on lobster yield and fishing strategy profit.
  • Market prices had a great impact on the ratio of fishing days
  • Maintenance price going up increased fishing days due to lower downtime.

Lobster license and boat size went hand-in-hand in lobster yield. If the captain had the cheapest lobster license, but the biggest boat, they would not be able to use the entire boat. Similarly, if they had the most expensive lobster license, but the smallest boat, they would not be able to fit all the lobsters on the boat.

The crew cut variable modeled crew motivation. If the crew cut was too high, then the added lobster yield may not be worth the cost to the fishing strategy profit. If it was too low, the lobster yield would go too low.

Market prices had a great impact on the ratio of fishing days. This is not a surprise.

The increase in maintenance price allows the boat to run with less downtime, enabling the boat to increase fishing days to meet the added demand for the DTC model and offset labor costs.

System Level Solution

To evaluate the profitability and viability of various commercial fishing strategies, we developed and integrated three key subsystems: Business Strategy, Boat Selection, and Fishing Strategy. Each subsystem models a critical aspect of the overall operation and is connected within a unified optimization framework. Integration was implemented through a master objective function, CombinedObjective, which takes a full decision vector as input and coordinates data flow between subsystems.

The Boat Selection subsystem models financial elements such as loan payments, licensing, maintenance schedules, labor costs, and revenues from product sales and optional boat rentals. This subsystem was enhanced to return detailed cost and revenue breakdowns for reporting and diagnostics, helping ensure the realism of financial assumptions.

Simultaneously, the Business Strategy subsystem models financial elements such as loan payments, licensing, maintenance schedules, labor costs, and revenues from product sales and optional boat rentals. This subsystem was enhanced to return detailed cost and revenue breakdowns for reporting and diagnostics, helping ensure the realism of financial assumptions.

The master CombinedObjective function integrates the three subsystems by:

  1. Passing boat characteristics to determine fuel costs and lobster pot limits
  2. Feeding those outputs into the Fishing subsystem to evaluate profit based on seasonal availability and effort allocation
  3. Evaluating financial feasibility through the Business subsystem, incorporating revenue from catches and rentals, as well as total operational costs
  4. Aggregating these elements into a single scalar objective (net profit), which is then minimized using MATLAB's Genetic Algorithm

By using a modular structure with clear data dependencies, we ensured each subsystem could be developed, tested, and refined independently while still contributing to an accurate system-level analysis. This integration enables holistic optimization over a complex, multi-variable decision space, ultimately guiding the selection of the most profitable fishing operation strategy.

The system-level optimization reveals critical nonlinearities that subsystem-level analysis overlooked. While subsystem optimization pushed maintenance costs and loan terms to their lower bounds (4,000 and 150,000 respectively), the integrated solution converged to more realistic values (5,000 for maintenance, and 162,000 for loans). This 25% increase in maintenance spending might correlate to a reduction in unexpected downtime (further explored below). The shift from wholesale to DTC—despite its higher labor costs—reflects the system's recognition of price premiums outweighing operational complexities when evaluated holistically. Most strikingly, we can see a complete elimination of the boat rental revenue.

The dramatic activity increases (3-4x more fishing days) stem from three system-level effects 1) changes in maintenance costs and scheduling, 2) adoption of the DTC model, and 3) eliminating rentals freed up the boat to be used for fishing.

The most important conclusions we can draw from these differences are that 1) boat rentals appeared profitable in isolation but created systematic inefficiencies, 2) Direct-to-customer may net more profit, despite the additional labor costs, 3) Optimal loan utilizations can buy equipment that have higher ROI, and 4) Increased maintenance costs can decrease downtime, which increases fishing days.

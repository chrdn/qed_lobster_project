Boat Selection

Vessel specifications and performance tradeoffs

Subsystem Description

Selecting the right lobster boat involves balancing competing priorities: fuel efficiency, storage capacity, seaworthiness, and cost. This subsystem models these tradeoffs using real-world data to replicate the decision-making process of a commercial fisherman. For instance, larger boats can carry more lobsters and gear but require bigger engines and consume more fuel. Similarly, hull design impacts performance in different ocean depths—planar hulls excel in shallow waters, while downeast hulls handle rougher, deeper seas but at a higher price. The subsystem’s goal is to minimize annual fuel costs while ensuring the boat meets minimum capacity requirements and stays within budget constraints for both upfront purchase (<$1.8M) and annual maintenance (<$180K)

Subsystem Modeling

The optimization considers four key variables: hull type, hull length, fuel type, and engine horsepower. All variables are discrete. Inputs from other subsystems—such as days at sea, operating hours, and maintenance costs—help contextualize the problem. For example, diesel engines, while more expensive upfront, often have lower long-term maintenance costs than gas engines, creating a tradeoff between immediate and recurring expenses.

Constraints play a critical role in guiding realistic solutions. The boat must have enough storage capacity to ensure profitability, which in turn drives the need for sufficient engine power. We incorporated a safety factor of 3 for horsepower to account for fully loaded conditions (lobsters, gear, fuel, etc.), ensuring the engine can handle real-world demands. Linear regression helped map empirical data on hull performance and fuel efficiency, while nonlinear relationships (e.g., engine power vs. fuel consumption) were modeled empirically.

Subsystem Optimization

To navigate the discrete design space, we employed MATLAB’s Genetic Algorithm (GA). Unlike gradient-based methods, GA excels at handling discrete variables and nonlinear problems, making it ideal for this application. The algorithm explored combinations of hulls, engines, and sizes, evaluating each against fuel costs, capacity, and budget limits.

After multiple runs, the GA consistently converged to the same optimal configuration: a 49' downeast hull with a 310 HP diesel engine. This setup strikes a balance between performance and cost, yielding an annual fuel expense of $76,538 and a catch capacity of 58,440 lobsters. The diesel engine’s higher initial cost is offset by its lower maintenance, while the downeast hull’s design ensures stability in deeper waters. Notably, the 300–400 HP range aligns with industry standards for a 50' lobster boat, validating the solution’s practicality.

Subsystem Analysis of Solution

The optimized solution reflects realistic compromises. The active constraints—minimum capacity and maximum budgets—directly shaped the outcome, confirming the model’s alignment with real-world priorities. For instance, the algorithm avoided undersized engines (which would violate the safety factor) or overly expensive hulls (which would breach the budget), demonstrating its ability to navigate tradeoffs.

However, the model has limitations. It assumes static fuel consumption rates, ignoring variables like weather or ocean currents. Future iterations could incorporate stochastic elements for finer accuracy. Additionally, expanding the hull-type options might reveal unexplored efficiencies. Despite this, the consistency of the GA’s results—even with its stochastic nature—suggests a robust optimum. The solution not only meets operational demands but also aligns with industry practices, underscoring its viability.

Lobster Fishing Optimization

System Introduction

For this project, we are optimizing the parameters for a successful 3 man lobster crew. We separate this system into three subsystems: 1) Business 2) Boat Selection 3) Fishing strategy.  The Business subsystem controls the budget and expected profit. Boat selection finds the best boat with respect to fuel cost given the budget. This fuel cost is then added to the net present value calculation of the Business subsystem. Fishing strategy takes the boat parameters from Boat selection and calculates the expected cash inflow. This cash inflow is also used by the Business submodel to obtain net present value.

Subsystem 1: Business

Subsystem Description:

The Business Subsystem models the financial viability of a commercial fishing operation. It explores tradeoffs between revenue streams (lobster sales, other fish sales, and off-season charter rental) and costs (boat purchase, maintenance, licensing, labor, and loan structure). We chose this subsystem to evaluate how financing, sales strategy, and asset utilization affect long-term profit, helping inform decisions at the business strategy level. A key focus was whether to prioritize wholesale or direct-to-customer sales, and how to allocate limited fishing and rental time to maximize profitability.

Subsystem Modeling:

We developed a detailed mathematical model that simulates the business operation over a season. The design variables include revenue stream toggles, maintenance interval, loan term, capital cost, licensing cost, rental usage, and sales strategy (wholesale vs. direct). The objective function computes total revenue minus all associated costs, including a realistic labor cost if direct-to-customer sales are selected. Constraints were added to enforce a maximum rental limit, minimum license cost, and budgetary limits on the boat purchase. The model also includes a soft penalty to discourage unrealistic configurations such as minimum maintenance or license costs.

Subsystem Optimization:

We used MATLAB's Genetic Algorithm to optimize our business model. The GA evaluated 10 variables, including 5 binary/integer decisions. It was configured with a population size of 500 and a max of 300 generations. The best solution achieved a net profit of over $7M and recommended using all available time: lobster fishing, fish sales, and 60 days of rental charters. The optimal strategy involved selling wholesale to avoid labor costs, using the cheapest boat possible, and minimizing loan term and maintenance interval.

Subsystem Analysis of Solution:

The optimized solution effectively balanced profitability and feasibility. The GA chose a strategy that filled the entire available season, prioritized simplicity via wholesale sales, and minimized costs wherever possible. However, the tendency to minimize maintenance and licensing suggests that the realism penalty may need to be strengthened. The results demonstrate that all three revenue streams contribute significantly to profit when well-coordinated, and validate the usefulness of the Genetic Algorithm in exploring high-dimensional tradeoffs. MATLAB was used for all modeling and optimization.

Subsystem 2: Boat Selection

Subsystem Description

Selecting the right lobster boat involves balancing competing priorities: fuel efficiency, storage capacity, seaworthiness, and cost. This subsystem models these tradeoffs using real-world data to replicate the decision-making process of a commercial fisherman. For instance, larger boats can carry more lobsters and gear but require bigger engines and consume more fuel. Similarly, hull design impacts performance in different ocean depths—planar hulls excel in shallow waters, while downeast hulls handle rougher, deeper seas but at a higher price. The subsystem’s goal is to minimize annual fuel costs while ensuring the boat meets minimum capacity requirements and stays within budget constraints for both upfront purchase (<$1.8M) and annual maintenance (<$180K)

Subsystem Modeling

The optimization considers four key variables: hull type, hull length, fuel type, and engine horsepower. All variables are discrete. Inputs from other subsystems—such as days at sea, operating hours, and maintenance costs—help contextualize the problem. For example, diesel engines, while more expensive upfront, often have lower long-term maintenance costs than gas engines, creating a tradeoff between immediate and recurring expenses.

Constraints play a critical role in guiding realistic solutions. The boat must have enough storage capacity to ensure profitability, which in turn drives the need for sufficient engine power. We incorporated a safety factor of 3 for horsepower to account for fully loaded conditions (lobsters, gear, fuel, etc.), ensuring the engine can handle real-world demands. Linear regression helped map empirical data on hull performance and fuel efficiency, while nonlinear relationships (e.g., engine power vs. fuel consumption) were modeled empirically.

Subsystem Optimization

To navigate the discrete design space, we employed MATLAB’s Genetic Algorithm (GA). Unlike gradient-based methods, GA excels at handling discrete variables and nonlinear problems, making it ideal for this application. The algorithm explored combinations of hulls, engines, and sizes, evaluating each against fuel costs, capacity, and budget limits.

After multiple runs, the GA consistently converged to the same optimal configuration: a 49' downeast hull with a 310 HP diesel engine. This setup strikes a balance between performance and cost, yielding an annual fuel expense of $76,538 and a catch capacity of 58,440 lobsters. The diesel engine’s higher initial cost is offset by its lower maintenance, while the downeast hull’s design ensures stability in deeper waters. Notably, the 300–400 HP range aligns with industry standards for a 50' lobster boat, validating the solution’s practicality.

Subsystem Analysis of Solution

The optimized solution reflects realistic compromises. The active constraints—minimum capacity and maximum budgets—directly shaped the outcome, confirming the model’s alignment with real-world priorities. For instance, the algorithm avoided undersized engines (which would violate the safety factor) or overly expensive hulls (which would breach the budget), demonstrating its ability to navigate tradeoffs.

However, the model has limitations. It assumes static fuel consumption rates, ignoring variables like weather or ocean currents. Future iterations could incorporate stochastic elements for finer accuracy. Additionally, expanding the hull-type options might reveal unexplored efficiencies. Despite this, the consistency of the GA’s results—even with its stochastic nature—suggests a robust optimum. The solution not only meets operational demands but also aligns with industry practices, underscoring its viability.

Subsystem 3: Fishing Strategy

Subsystem Description

The Fishing strategy Subsystem models the tradeoffs between fishing for different species. This subsystem captures real-world decision-making by incorporating license costs, current market prices, and realistic payment methods with a curve to measure production against salary. Key considerations include:

  • Lobster vs. Shellfish vs. Cod: Different target species require different equipment, licenses, work hours, and commute times. These species also peak at different points in the fishing season.
  • Crew cut vs. Production: If deckhands and crew members are paid a larger percentage of the total profit, they may be more productive and catch their daily limit faster. This can lead to more efficient commute times, and lower operational costs for gas and food.
  • Maintenance and Operational Costs: Different fishing strategies also require different baits which vary in costs, and carry different maintenance costs as well. Cod fishing for example is much less costly to get into, and less costly to maintain as there is less equipment involved compared to something like lobster fishing. Traps need to be serviced and meet strict regulations, and buoys need to similarly be maintained and follow regulation.

The subsystem’s primary objective is to maximize daily profit over the course of  a fishing season while maintaining:

  1. Boat capacity above a minimum threshold.

  2. Budget constraints: Boat price ≤ 1.8M, annual maintenance ≤ 180K.
  3. Engine power constraint: Horsepower must be sufficient to propel the fully loaded boat (factoring in lobsters, gear, and a safety factor of 3).

Modeling Approach:

  • Linear regression was used to map real-world data for hull performance, fuel efficiency, and engine requirements.
  • Nonlinear relationships (e.g., hull type vs. seaworthiness, engine power vs. fuel consumption) were captured empirically.

Subsystem Optimization

We used MATLAB’s Genetic Algorithm (GA) due to:

  1. Discrete variables: Hull type and fuel type are non-continuous.
  2. Nonlinearity: Boat performance tradeoffs are inherently nonlinear.
  3. Exploration capability: GA efficiently searches high-dimensional design spaces.

Optimal Solution:
The GA converged consistently to the same solution, indicating robustness:

  • 49' downeast hull (optimal balance of seaworthiness and cost)
  • 310 HP diesel engine (lower maintenance costs than gas, sufficient power for loaded weight)
  • Fuel cost: $76,538 annually
  • Annual catch: 58,440 lobsters

Validation:

  • Exploring online forums, many lobster fishermen exhibited similar fishing strategies and profit margins
  • There are real considerations of crew percentages used as incentives to increase productivity

Subsystem Analysis of Solution

The optimized solution effectively balances work hours, licensing, distribution and operational costs:

  • Active constraints: Capacity and budget constraints drive the solution, validating the model’s realism.
  • Consistency: Repeated GA runs converged to the same optimum, suggesting a global minimum.
  • Tradeoffs captured:
  • There is a point at which yield cannot increase enough to justify an increase in crew incentives
  • Lobster fishing does tend to be the most profitable due to the high market price of lobster compared to other target species

Limitations & Improvements:

  • The model assumes linear fuel consumption rates; actual conditions (weather, currents) could refine accuracy.
  • The weight of the yield is not considered in commute times and fuel consumption
  • There is no consideration for natural disasters that could reduce yield and limit the fishing season length
  • There is also no consideration for the likelihood of injury, and equipment damage such as a net for shellfish being destroyed on ship wreckage among other possibilities.
